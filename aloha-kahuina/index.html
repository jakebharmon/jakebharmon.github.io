A Cover Letter for Kahuina Consulting

Jake Harmon

Public Health Associate — Application to Kahuina Consulting

“Kahuina is Hawaiian for bringing people together.” I’ve spent my career learning that the best public health happens at the places where people, data, and systems converge.

The Journey

Introduction

Before I ever opened a dataset, I learned epidemiology from the back of an ambulance.

As an EMT with DeKalb County Fire Rescue, I responded to 911 calls across one of metro Atlanta’s most diverse counties. The clinical work was intense — patient assessments, chief complaints, care reports filed under protocol. But the patterns I noticed had nothing to do with any single call. They were systemic.

I started recognizing faces. The same individuals calling 911 week after week — not because of emergencies, but because the emergency system was the only door that stayed open for them. I helped form a community paramedicine program that documented these frequent, non-emergency callers so we could connect them with the alternative support they actually needed: primary care, social services, stable housing.

Then there were the opioid calls. I carried naloxone and used it. But I also carried information — drug treatment resources, rehab program contacts — because I learned quickly that the window between resuscitation and discharge was sometimes the only moment a person would accept help. That work taught me something I carry into every project today: data is only as valuable as the human connection that delivers it.

That realization is what pulled me from emergency response into public health. I wanted to work upstream — not just on the patients arriving by ambulance, but on the systems, the surveillance, and the infrastructure that determine whether communities thrive or fall through the cracks.

The Work

Technical Portfolio

I build tools that help public health agencies make better decisions with their data. Below are three projects that represent how I think about the intersection of epidemiology, data science, and public health practice.

Data Privacy & Governance

K-Anonymity Simulation for CSTE

I wrote this simulation for CSTE’s LEARN course (Course 3.1: Data Privacy) to demonstrate the distorting effect of k-anonymity as a de-identification technique. The project creates four progressive scenarios showing the delicate balance between making data publicly available and upholding HIPAA protections — with 3D Plotly visualizations that make the tradeoffs tangible.

R Plotly Simulation HIPAA

View on GitHub

Infectious Disease Modeling

Infectious Disease Modeling Paper

Coursework under Dr. Gerardo Chowell at Georgia State University, this Quarto manuscript applies compartmental modeling and Bayesian methods (Stan) to infectious disease dynamics. Built as a fully reproducible research document — the kind of transparent, version-controlled analytical workflow that modern public health demands.

R Quarto Stan Bayesian renv

View on GitHub

Health Equity & Geospatial Analysis

Vaccines for Children Provider Map

Built with the Southern Alliance for Public Health Leadership, this interactive Leaflet map examines the relationship between Vaccines for Children (VFC) provider locations and eligible child populations across Georgia and South Carolina. By layering ACS child poverty data by ZCTA against VFC provider proximity, the map reveals where high-need communities have the least access to free childhood immunizations.

Health Equity in Practice

Centering Communities

Bilingual Case Interviewing

At the Georgia Department of Public Health, I conduct disease case interviews in both English and Spanish, entering data into the State Electronic Notifiable Disease Surveillance System (SENDSS). This isn’t just a language skill on a resume — it’s the difference between a completed investigation and a missed case. For Spanish-speaking communities navigating a disease investigation, having an interviewer who can communicate directly reduces barriers, builds trust, and produces higher-quality surveillance data. Public health data is only as complete as the communities it reaches.

Health Equity Lens

Every tool I build, every report I automate, and every interview I conduct is shaped by a simple question: who is this serving, and who might it be leaving out? From mapping VFC deserts in rural Georgia to supporting opioid response in DeKalb County, I approach public health practice through an asset-based framework — identifying what communities have, not just what they lack.

Cross-Cultural Collaboration

With Pacific Island epidemiologists at the CSTE Annual Conference

Working at CSTE has given me the privilege of collaborating with epidemiologists and public health professionals from across U.S. states and territories — including colleagues from Pacific Island jurisdictions who bring irreplaceable perspective on surveillance capacity, data sovereignty, and health systems that serve geographically dispersed populations. These conversations have shaped how I think about data modernization: not as a one-size-fits-all technology upgrade, but as an adaptive process that must honor local context.
